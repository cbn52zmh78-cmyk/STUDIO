## THE 4 TONES

Mandarin is a tonal language. The same syllable said in a different tone = a completely different word. Getting the tone right matters.

| Tone | Name | Mark | Shape | Sound like... |
|------|------|------|-------|---------------|
| 1st | First tone | ā | Flat line — | Hold a steady "ahh" at the doctor |
| 2nd | Second tone | á | Rising / | "Huh?" in English — voice goes up |
| 3rd | Third tone | ǎ | Dip then rise ∨ | "Well..." doubtfully — down then back up |
| 4th | Fourth tone | à | Falling \ | A firm "No." — voice drops sharply |
| Neutral | Neutral tone | a (no mark) | Short dot | Light and quick — barely there |

**The tone mark sits on the vowel:**  
mā · má · mǎ · mà · ma  
(They mean: mother · hemp · horse · scold · [question particle] — same sound, 5 meanings)

## A NOTE ON NǏ HǍO

You might expect two Tone 3 syllables to both dip-and-rise. In natural speech, when two Tone 3 syllables appear in a row, the FIRST one rises instead (becomes Tone 2). So **nǐ hǎo** is actually spoken as **ní hǎo**.

You don't need to change the written pinyin — the tone marks stay as written. This is called **tone sandhi** and it happens automatically as you speak more naturally. Don't overthink it; just listen and imitate.
