CORPUS ANCHOR
- Man'yōshū (万葉集, c. 759 CE): 4,516 poems compiled under imperial patronage; uses Man'yōgana (Chinese characters as phonetic symbols) — primary phonological source
- Kokinshū (古今和歌集, 905 CE): imperially commissioned waka anthology; Heian standard
- Genji Monogatari (源氏物語, c. 1000–1012 CE): Lady Murasaki Shikibu; world's earliest novel by many accounts
- Makura no Sōshi (枕草子, c. 996–1002 CE): Sei Shōnagon; prose
- Buddhist texts / ritual readings: preserve archaic onyomi pronunciations standardised across centuries
- Held digitally: National Institute of Japanese Language and Linguistics (NINJAL), Man'yōshū full text corpus

HISTORICAL / LINGUISTIC SIGNIFICANCE
- Man'yōgana reveals an 8-vowel system (vs modern 5 vowels) lost by c. 1000 CE
- Classical Japanese grammar radically different from modern: verb endings (mizenkei, ren'yōkei, etc.), classical negation (zu), older copulas (nari, tari)
- Comparative Ryukyuan (Okinawan dialects) confirms archaic Japanese pitch features
- Continues in formal, poetic, and religious registers; waka poetry composed in classical grammar to this day
