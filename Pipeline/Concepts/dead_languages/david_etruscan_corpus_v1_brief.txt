CORPUS ANCHOR
- ~13,000 inscriptions on stone, bronze, terracotta; c. 700–100 BCE; across Etruria (modern Tuscany, Umbria, Lazio)
- Liber Linteus (Linen Book): only surviving pre-Roman book; linen wrapping of an Egyptian mummy (Louvre-Lens / Zagreb Archaeological Museum, inv. NS 200); c. 250 BCE; religious calendar text
- Pyrgi Tablets (c. 500 BCE): three gold plates — two Etruscan, one Phoenician — bilingual dedication from sanctuary of Pyrgi; held at Museo Nazionale Etrusco di Villa Giulia, Rome
- Corpus Inscriptionum Etruscarum (CIE): scholarly corpus of all known inscriptions
- Cippus Perusinus: bilingual Etruscan–Latin boundary stone, Perugia

DEMONSTRATION LINE
mi nunar thamuce velus hulchniesi
Etruscan dedicatory inscription (generic form attested across multiple inscriptions)
Gloss: "I (am) the gift made for Vel Hulchnie"
Nature: ATTESTED Etruscan alphabet (direct phonetic); vocabulary partially UNDERSTOOD (mi = I; nunar = gift [confirmed via context]; thamuce = made [confirmed via parallels]); semantics of some elements still debated

ATTESTATION STATUS
Attested: Alphabet / phonology (largely confirmed via Latin alphabet transmission and loanwords)
Partial: Grammar (case endings, verbal morphology identified; syntax debated)
Unknown: Full vocabulary (~30% of attested words remain untranslated)
Unknown: Language family (isolate; Tyrsenian hypothesis with Raetic and Lemnian; not established)

HISTORICAL / LINGUISTIC SIGNIFICANCE
- The Latin alphabet descends from the Etruscan alphabet, which descended from Western Greek
- Etruscan loanwords entered Latin: persona (mask), histrio (actor), atrium, lanista (gladiator trainer)
- Roman ritual practice, augury, and architectural vocabulary deeply Etruscan-influenced (haruspicy)
- The Pyrgi Tablets are among the most important bilingual texts in Western epigraphy
- Liber Linteus represents a unique survival: the only pre-Roman Italian book in any language
