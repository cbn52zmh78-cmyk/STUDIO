# What We Changed — *The Keys* (rough draft → developed screenplay)

Same story, same beats, same emotional ending. Every change below is craft, not
plot — the proof is that we kept the writer's intent and raised the execution.

## At a glance

| | Rough draft | Developed draft |
|---|---|---|
| Format | Prose paragraphs, inconsistent cues | Standard screenplay (sluglines, action, cues) |
| Words | ~430 | ~330 (−23%) |
| Dialogue mode | On-the-nose (states feelings) | Subtext (feelings underneath) |
| Backstory delivery | Narrated flashback | Two spoken lines, no flashback |
| Stage direction | Camera/"we see" in prose | Behavior + image; camera-neutral |
| Errors | "wether", "your"/"you're", "Karens" | Corrected |

## Line-level changes

| # | Issue in rough draft | Principle | Fix in developed draft |
|---|----------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| 1 | No scene heading; opens in prose | **Format** | `INT. ROBERT'S KITCHEN - LATE AFTERNOON` slugline + screenplay layout |
| 2 | "Robert is an old man, 78 years old, and he is sitting…" | **Action economy** | "ROBERT, 78, sits at the table reading a folded newspaper he isn't reading." — age + character in one image |
| 3 | Parentheticals carry the emotion: `(angrily, but also sad)`, `(stubborn)`, `(crying now)` | **Acting is the actor's job** | Parentheticals cut; emotion shown through behavior (turning the paper face-down, smoothing it twice) |
| 4 | "Dad, we need to talk about something important. You know I love you…the doctor says your eyes are too bad…" | **Subtext over on-the-nose** | "It freezes." / "How are the boys." / "Dad. Dr. Ellison called me." — the bad news lands in three words |
| 5 | "I will basically be a prisoner and I will have nothing left." | **Show the fear, don't state it** | Replaced by the "Sundays / cemetery" demand — reveals the real fear is abandonment, not immobility |
| 6 | Flashback to teaching her to drive ("The camera shows a flashback…") | **Stay in the scene; lose the camera** | Cut entirely; the past arrives as dialogue: "Your mother and I took the coast road in that car. Before you." |
| 7 | "She holds out her hand for the keys." (twice the scene begs) | **Power through restraint** | "She doesn't hold out her hand. She just waits." Karen sets *her own* keys down first — a mirror, not a demand |
| 8 | Ending narrates the theme: "family is complicated but love wins in the end." | **Trust the image** | He sets his keys beside hers, doesn't let go, then lifts his hand. She takes off her coat and stays. Theme dramatized, never stated |
| 9 | Sensory world absent (white kitchen of exposition) | **Specificity** | "One coffee cup in the rack." / dented fender + duct-taped mailbox seen, never discussed / school bus hisses past |
| 10 | Spelling/grammar: "wether", "your asking", "Karens" | **Proofing** | Corrected throughout |

## What we deliberately kept

- The premise, the two characters, the single location, and the keys as the
  central object.
- The honeymoon / coast-road detail (the writer's best original line of
  backstory) — promoted from buried prose to a spoken beat.
- The bittersweet resolution: the keys change hands and the relationship
  survives.

## Net effect

Shorter, castable, and playable. The rough draft *tells* you a sad thing
happened; the developed draft *stages* it so an audience feels it — and it now
reads in correct screenplay format that a director, line producer, and legal
gate can all work from.
