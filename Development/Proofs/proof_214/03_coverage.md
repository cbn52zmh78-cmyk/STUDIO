## Logline

When a daughter drives out to take her widowed father's car keys after a minor
accident, both must negotiate — without ever saying so — the moment the child
becomes the parent.

## Synopsis

Karen, 45, a nurse, arrives at the home of her 78-year-old father Robert under
the pretext of dropping off food. The evidence of his failing eyesight — a
dented fender, a duct-taped mailbox — sits unspoken between them. Robert
deflects with small talk and with the dignity of a forty-one-year driving
record; Karen counters not with argument but with patience, placing her own
keys on the table as a quiet mirror. Robert surrenders his keys only after
extracting the thing he actually fears losing: not mobility, but his daughter's
regular presence ("Sundays. You take me to the cemetery Sundays."). The scene
closes on Karen taking off her coat and staying — the transaction reframed as
a renewed obligation rather than a loss.

## Comments

**Strengths.** This is a deceptively simple two-hander that earns its emotion
through restraint. The central conflict — an adult child stripping a parent of
independence — is universal and immediately legible, and the writer wisely
keeps it to one location, two people, and a single physical object (the keys)
that carries the entire dramatic weight. The dialogue runs almost entirely on
subtext: characters discuss food, grandchildren, and a forty-one-year commute
while the real negotiation happens underneath. The "Sundays" beat is the
strongest moment — it reveals that Robert's true terror is abandonment, not
immobility, and converts a scene about taking into a scene about promising. The
key visual (two sets of keys side by side; he doesn't let go right away) is
precisely the kind of image-driven storytelling that reads on the page and
plays on screen.

**Weaknesses.** As submitted it is a self-contained scene, not a story — there
is no surrounding feature or short to assess for arc, and the cemetery reveal,
while effective, is the only turn. A full draft would need to test whether this
register sustains across a longer runtime. Minor: the casserole "ticking/
settling" sound cue is a nice touch but flirts with over-direction; one
ambient beat is enough.

**Marketability / production.** Single interior, two roles, no effects, no
period or genre cost — an ideal calling-card scene or festival-short anchor,
and a clean fit for the STUDIO documentary-prestige / two-hander capability.
Castable at any budget.

## Verdict

**CONSIDER the scene · RECOMMEND the writer.** The page-level craft —
subtext, economy, image — is at professional sample standard. Advance to
short-film development or use as a writing sample; commission a full draft to
evaluate sustained structure.
